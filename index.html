About Me

Welcome! I am a Ph.D. candidate in Political Science at Yale University. My research focuses on how states regulate intimate relationships and construct hierarchies of familial belonging.

I am interested in the complexities of modern human life. In research, these ideas explore state regulation in private life, feminist theory, and law and society. Outside of work, this usually means an enduring interest in celebrity gossip, reality TV and literary fiction.

My PhD dissertation introduces the Intimacy Contract, a framework that illuminates how some intimate relationships receive legal entitlements while others are rendered dispensable. This pattern becomes evident when we compare marriages to extra-marital relationships, parent-child relations to nanny-child relations -- and so on.

This is a topic that I grew interested due to a fortuitious confluence of factors. In 2022, I spent three months in the British Library archives in London when I was studying the lives of British colonial policemen. Their private documents showed (explicit) accounts of their deep, intimate relationships with 'local' i.e., Indian women, but never thought to call them their 'wives'. Coincidentally, I also happened to be reading content that involved polyamorous relationships, like Tawaifnama, Jennifer Wilson's New Yorker article on Polyamory and repeated references to concubines in a variety of Political Science literature.

My research involves a combination of computational, qualitative, and quantitative methods to study extramarital relationships in India and non-biological caregiving in the U.S. I've recently become an LLM and AI enthusiast, and believe it can be a powerful aid to research in the social sciences.

Prior to Yale, I received an M.A. in Social Sciences from the University of Chicago and an integrated M.A. in Development Studies from IIT Madras. I grew up in Bangalore and now live in Brooklyn with my dog, daughter and partner.

Research

Dissertation

  • Job Market Paper: The Intimacy Contract in Action

    How do states decide which intimate relationships are “family”?

    In India, relationships outside legal, monogamous marriage lack legal standing. Yet appellate courts sometimes extend legal entitlements to women in extramarital relationships. To understand these patterns, I develop the Intimacy Contract: a conceptual framework illuminating how states reward relationships resembling their ideological vision of marriage. This framework addresses a blind spot in social science scholarship that neglects how states extend and deny entitlements to relationships. Using large language models (LLMs) with human oversight, I analyze 2,721 Indian appellate court cases from 2000 to 2024. I find that when relationships exhibit a conjugal life, a traditional gender dynamic, and shared religiosity, courts grant recognition in 56 percent of cases and when these are absent, the rate drops to 22 percent. This stark difference reflects a broader ideological logic through which states, across various contexts, regulate intimate life.

    (Jul 2025)
